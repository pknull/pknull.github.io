7th Continent Strategy

7th Continent — Strategy (Tonight’s Session)

Setup: 3-4 players, single evening, starter curse, no prior notes. Loss pattern: Action deck reaches zero → forced to draw from discard pile → curse card drawn → instant loss. Diagnosis: No sustainable food/recycle loop, no hand discipline, no notes, exploration without intent.

Synthesis from: UltraBoardGames rules, UltraBoardGames glossary, Serious Poulp FAQ, Tobold’s How to Win, Tobold’s Non-Spoiler Hints, Elusive Meeple Strategy Tips.

The Core Mechanic

Your action deck is your life. Every action draws cards. The entire game reduces to a single rule: don’t let the action deck reach zero.

The death condition is narrower than it might seem. Two things have to be true at the same time:

  1. The action deck is empty right now (not “has been empty before” — actually zero cards)
  2. You’re being forced to draw cards (during an action’s Cost step)

When both conditions hold, you shuffle the discard pile and draw randomly from it. If you draw a curse card from that pile, you lose immediately.

Curse cards in the discard are dormant as long as the action deck has any cards in it. You can look through the discard freely. Curse cards only become lethal in the moment the action deck has bottomed out and you’re being forced to draw from discard.

Curse cards drawn during normal play (while the action deck still has cards) just trigger their printed effect (fail the check, cause a state, etc.) and go to discard. Annoying but not lethal.

The single rule: keep the action deck above zero. Eating moves cards from the discard pile back into the action deck. Eat enough to refill faster than you draw → action deck never hits zero → you can play indefinitely. Stop eating → deck empties → first forced discard-draw is potentially game-ending.

(Edge case from the official FAQ: when only 3 cards remain in the action deck, a revealed curse card has no effect — a small buffer at the very end.)

The 7 Steps of an Action — Memorize This

Per @Clipper in the Easy to Forget Rules thread, every action resolves in this exact order. Knowing the order unlocks tactical timing:

Step What happens
1 Items — choose brown-icon abilities to use
2 Cost — determine cards to draw from action deck
3 Result — count successes (stars)
4 Skill — one player keeps one drawn skill card (ignoring hand size for now)
5 Discard — discard used cards + items that lost their last die
6 Consequence — apply pass or fail effect
7 Hand Size Limit — discard down to hand limit

Why this order matters

  • You always get to keep one skill card per action, no matter the hand size, temporarily. You can then USE that card during step 6 (Consequence) — which puts it in discard naturally and means you don’t have to discard it at step 7. Effectively: grab card → use card → no penalty.
  • Item durability hits at step 1, not after. Decide whether to use the item before drawing.
  • Hand limit only enforces at step 7. You can be over hand size during steps 4-6 as long as you’ve resolved by step 7.
  • Exception for item-swap actions (giving items between players, 2-card draw): you do NOT get to keep a skill card. Both cards go straight to discard.

Movement Cost Discount — Big for 3-4 Players

Per the same thread (@Clipper):

“Non-custom characters involved in moving to a Terrain card with at least one character or a fire token present reduce the cost for each of the characters that is moving.”

Translation: every non-custom character has a passive (brown icon) ability that triggers on the Walk action — if your destination terrain already has a character OR a fire token on it, you get -1 movement cost per moving character.

Example with your group

  • 4 players moving together to a terrain with a fire token
  • Each character contributes -1 to the cost
  • Total movement cost reduced by 4 cards

Tactical implications

  • Fire tokens are mobile infrastructure. Place fires strategically along your route — every party movement back to that node costs 1 less per character.
  • Don’t split the party. Splitting fragments this discount. A party of 4 moving together saves 4 cards per move; one player moving alone saves only 1.
  • Custom characters have different abilities that stack differently — check your character cards before assuming.

Movement vs Permanent Events — What Blocks You

  • Terrain cards do NOT block movement, even if they have weather, rockworms, or other hostile state cards on them. You can pass through ignored.
  • Permanent Event cards DO block movement. If a permanent event bridges two locations (like the bridge between the starter islands), you have to move to one side, deal with the event, then move from the other side. You can’t leap over.
  • Officially confirmed by devs: rockworms and weather effects on intermediate tiles are ignored when moving past.

Rules Gotchas — Read Before Tonight

These are the common rules errors that quietly cost games. From the BGG “Some Gotchas” thread, including posts from designers @sgbeal and @nerman8r. Read these once before the game starts.

Movement & Action

  • Movement is not adjacent-only. A Move action (four arrows) lets you travel to any exposed terrain connected by terrain cards alone — permanent event cards block the path, but anything reachable through terrain is reachable in one action.
  • You can only take actions via white icons. Brown icons are passive effects that trigger when the corresponding white action is happening. You can’t “use” a brown icon on its own.
  • You can’t create fire out of nothing — need a white fire-making icon available (terrain or crafted fire-starter item).
  • Always pick an active player per action. They face specific consequences on some cards. Default: whoever drew the cards.
  • Giving items to other players costs 2 action cards. Not free — factor into planning.

Cards and Text

  • Stars on cards count ONLY when drawn from the action deck. Cards in your hand apply their printed effects, not star counts. (Common mistake.)
  • READ THE BACKS OF ADVENTURE CARDS. Designer @sgbeal: “Failing to do so can literally break the game, leaving it in an unwinnable state.” He lost a whole day of play to this.
  • Green-to-gold icon = BANISH the green card, replace with gold. The icon doesn’t visually scream “banish” — easy to miss. (Rulebook page 17.)

Items & Crafting

  • Stacking is keyword-independent. Any item can stack on any item. Keywords only determine whether dice/durability combine when stacking.
  • Only the top card’s keywords matter for combining dice. Match a keyword on top = durability adds (max 6). No match = stacks but durability doesn’t compound.
  • Item durability decrements at the START of an action, before cards are drawn. You can choose not to apply item bonuses.
  • Items at 0 durability aren’t discarded until step 5 of action resolution — you get the benefit this action, then they go.
  • Multiple item-column effects stack. If your item has two Attack cards in its column and you do an Attack action, both bonuses apply if you want.
  • Crafted items can go to ANY participating character’s inventory, not just the crafter.
  • Character-specific skill cards are locked to that character’s hand. But if they craft it into an item, the item can go to anyone.
  • Food is an item, goes to INVENTORY (not hand). You must place it in inventory before eating, even if you plan to eat it immediately — this is how hand/inventory limits enforce.

States

  • Only one copy of each state. Drawing a duplicate = resolve the state’s effect, return the old state to box, add the new one.
  • Failing an action with 2+ characters gives the active character the Paranoid state. Group failure has a punishment multiplier.
  • You can take a skill card into hand whether you succeed OR fail an action. @briezee was only doing it on success — missed a bunch of skill cards.

XP & Decks

  • XP transition: When green XP cards run out, you get golden XP instead; green cards go back to box.
  • How to spend XP is revealed during play, not in the rulebook. Don’t worry if it’s unclear at start.
  • Past pile = SORT, don’t shuffle. Pre-sort by card type and number range for speed. When a card ID is depleted, you just return all copies of that ID to the box.
  • Curse cards stay in the action deck permanently even after lifting the curse. Only the clue card gets banished when a curse lifts. Playing multiple curses means permanent difficulty.

The Survival Loop

HUNT/FISH → COOK AT FIRE → EAT → recycles action cards from discard back to deck

This is the engine. Cooking doubles food value — never eat raw if a fire is available. Without this loop, you decline. With it, you can sustain indefinitely.

Required infrastructure (Hour 1 priority): - Hunting weapon (spear, bow, sling) - Fire-starting capability - Known hunting/fishing location near a fire source

Tobold’s rule, emphasized: “Unless your discard pile is empty, you’ll always want to hunt first, explore other stuff later.” Whenever a terrain shows animal tracks with a hunt action — hunt before doing anything else on that tile, every time.

Hunting locations deplete with use. Rotate spots. Saving the game can prune used hunting cards from circulation, refreshing future yields. Resting at hunting spots can also refresh them (per the Rest section above).

Hunt Discipline

The BGG community offers three perspectives on hunt timing:

  • “Wait until discard is 10+ cards before hunting” (@Evan E) — don’t waste a hunt on a near-empty discard
  • “Stockpile food in inventory and hunt when prepared” (@nobody82b) — woven basket + 3-4 food cards; when deck runs low, eat → hunt with full deck for safer draws
  • “Never hunt with a thin deck or bad gear” (@Achti) — going to hunt without enough cards to draw or good weapons gets you killed

Practical rule: hunt early when prepared; carry food forward in a basket if you have one; don’t hunt panicked with a thin deck.

Other hunt rules: - Never hunt while bleeding/wounded unless you can draw 4+ cards safely - Always cook before eating when fire is available — doubles the recovery - Designate one player as the food-carrier in 3-4 player games — they hold the basket and food stack

Hand vs Inventory — Two Separate Limits (rule confusion that loses games)

Per the Serious Poulp Maxed Items thread, this catches new groups:

Pile Contains Limit
Hand Skill cards + Bonus cards + State cards Skill+Bonus limited per player count (shown on Satchel & Journal). State doesn’t count, max 1 of each state type.
Inventory Item cards only (the things you carry) Combined-item limit per player count (also on Satchel card). Solo = 4 combined items.

Item cards have two flavors: - Item-only symbol (right corner) → goes directly to inventory when found - Item + Skill symbol → goes to hand first; you must perform a craft action later to convert it into an inventory item

Hand-size enforcement is at end of action (step 7). Excess Skill/Bonus cards get discarded for free. Inventory limit is enforced immediately — you can’t overflow inventory and “tend to it later.”

Hand discipline (still critical)

Implication of free hand discard: A card you played for marginal benefit is strictly better than a card discarded for hand limit. Same outcome (card hits discard), but playing it gave you the effect.

Rules tonight: 1. Before each action, glance at your hand. If you’re at limit, play something down — even a small effect is free value. 2. Don’t hoard “I might need this” cards past hand size. The game punishes hoarding. 3. Cards with cycle effects (Examine the Notes, Learning by Doing, similar) — use them aggressively. Tobold flags hoarding these as a documented loss pattern.

Inventory discipline

  1. Hunt with empty inventory slots. A 5-meat hunt with full inventory means discarding food. Ouch.
  2. Stack food onto food-keyword items only. Stacking food under a club doesn’t share keywords — wastes the food’s stamina-recovery potential.
  3. Coordinate inventory across the party. With 3-4 players, each player has tight inventory (likely 2 combined items each). Crafter holds craft items, hunter holds weapons, food-carrier holds food. Don’t all carry the same things.
  4. Stacking — when you find an item, you can combine it with an existing inventory item. Same keyword = durability adds together. Different keywords = item still stacks but durability doesn’t compound the same way.
  5. You can’t simply discard one card from a stack unless a card effect tells you to. To free up inventory you discard the whole stack.

Action Card Discipline

Every Move = 1 card. Every Explore = 2+ cards. Every check = N cards where N is the difficulty number.

Pool skill cards before drawing: matching skill cards laid down replace card draws 1-for-1. This is the single biggest deck saver across all sources.

Hard rules tonight: 1. Pool skill cards on every check ≥4. Lay down before any player draws. 2. No Explore without a stated reason. “What are we hoping to find?” answered out loud first. 3. Sun/cancel cards reserved for high-cost checks, not exploration. 4. Don’t redo solved card numerics. That’s what notes are for. 5. Walk away from bad-odds checks unless the reward is critical. “Maybe useful” isn’t a reason to spend 8 cards. 6. Some checks have multiple outcome versions — if you fail and the failure card is discarded, the next attempt has better odds. Worth knowing for cards that resist on first try.

Resource Placement Tactics

Fire is more than cooking. Strategic fire placement creates dual-purpose hubs:

  • Fire on a 1-cost-exit terrain reduces movement cost — when you stop on the fire terrain mid-traversal, you separate the movement into two cost steps (drawing 1 card per step instead of 2 at once). Multi-player benefit.
  • Fire enables “free crafting” — you can stop at a fire terrain without paying a build action’s full cost; use the fire as the resource for crafting items that need it. Per @robinz: “It’s ‘free’ in card cost to ‘stop off’ on the card with the fire, you can build items there using its resource, rather than being forced to either wait till you find that resource again or overpay for the build action.”

Implication: when you secure a hunting spot near a fire, you also secure your crafting workshop. That terrain becomes home base.

Crafting — the hidden hand-economy benefit

Crafting an item turns a Skill card (in your hand, counts against hand limit) into an Item card (in your inventory, separate limit). Per @Evan E:

“A crafted item frees up a slot in your hand for more cards.”

You’re not just gaining a tool — you’re transferring a card from a tight pile to a separate pile. This is a hand-economy multiplier, not just a power-up. Craft early-game even if you don’t immediately need the item.

Stack durability trick: if the top card of your item stack has two keywords, you can add cards matching either keyword to extend durability. Pick top-of-stack items with two keywords for maximum repair flexibility.

Repair via discard effects: per @Achti, you can free up stack room to “repair” items by using “discard card” effects on individual cards in a stack. Specific stacks can be effectively maintained indefinitely with the right card combos.

Stay Together (multi-player principle)

Per official rules: “A good winning strategy in multiplayer is for all players to complete actions together.” This is a 7C-specific design quirk — splitting up doesn’t multiply progress, it fragments your card pool and prevents skill-card pooling.

With 3-4 players: move as a group. Act on the same tile. Pool everything. Splitting the party is a board-game reflex from other co-ops; it does not apply here.

Examine Terrain Artwork (and the Backs of Cards)

The artwork on terrain tiles is functional, not decorative. Look for: - Hidden numbers that unlock tile replacement options or alternate actions - Plants, tracks, environmental clues that inform decisions - Visual references that connect to other cards or quest items

Habit: when a new terrain hits the table, the note-taker scans the art for a few seconds before anyone declares an action.

READ THE BACKS OF CARDS. Per @robertopellizini in the BGG first-game thread: players have gotten stuck for hours because they missed important instructions printed on the back of a card. Flip cards over before assuming they’re decorative — especially curse clue cards.

Use the magnifying glass that comes in the box — multiple players in the BGG thread mention small details only readable with it.

Pay attention to flavor text. It often telegraphs objectives, danger, or solutions. Per @CheddarLimbo: “Every time we thought something was unfair or random, upon closer examination we found that there were clues we’d missed that made sense.”

Watching the Action Deck

The action deck count is the single most important number at the table. Note-taker calls it every 15-20 min, or before any check ≥6 cards.

Action deck remaining What it means What to do
Healthy (>50%) Safe to take risks, explore freely Continue normal play
Thinning (~30%) Death window approaching if not addressed Plan a hunt → cook → eat cycle within next few actions
Critical (<15%) One bad check could empty it Stop everything else, eat now if you have food, route to a hunting spot if you don’t
Empty Death cliff — every Cost-step draw could end the game Save immediately if you can; eat or hunt is the only path back

Curse cards in discard is a secondary metric. It only matters as the action deck approaches empty — at that moment, more curse cards in the discard means a higher probability that the first forced draw ends the run. While the action deck is healthy, the discard’s curse-card count is academic.

State counts per character is the other secondary metric — see the State Cards section. State accumulation forces extra discards from the action deck as new states are gained, indirectly accelerating the death cliff.

Rest — Mechanics

Rest is narrow and context-specific. From designer Stephan Beal on BGG:

“A rest action often does not advance the story in any way, but may only provide a way to get rid of a certain State card. Some places with a Rest icon may offer ‘success’ and ‘fail’ texts, but not all do. Those which do not are basically ‘no-ops’ unless you have a card (typically a State card) which is affected by resting.”

Rest is not a generic deck-recovery mechanic. It has specific, narrow uses:

The four real uses of Rest

  1. Removing the Tired state. If you have a Tired state card, the card itself has a rest action printed on it. Take the action → discard the Tired card. Each player resolves their own Tired separately when using their own card.

  2. Hunting spot refresh. Per @vertseper: “Rest in hunting spots return the golden card and allow you to pull a green card if they are still available.” This is the sustainability use — if a hunting spot has been used, resting there can refresh it for another hunt. Big deal for sustaining the survival loop.

  3. Specific terrain rest locations with success/fail outcomes — varies by location. Some grant XP-purchase access to advanced skill cards via the 000 card (per Tobold). Many are no-ops without specific state cards or items.

  4. Time expansion interactions (only relevant if you’re using the Classic Game / time module).

Rest icon types

  • Black-on-white rest icon = rest action is doable here (specific rest location, or your own Tired state card)
  • White-on-brown rest icon = passive effect that triggers when a rest action is performed via another source — does NOT enable resting on its own

Practical rules for tonight

  • Don’t expect rest to save a thinning deck. That’s eating’s job. Rest doesn’t return cards to the action deck.
  • Use rest at depleted hunting spots to refresh them — major sustainability win.
  • If anyone gets Tired, they can rest off the Tired state at any rest-enabled location (or via the state card’s own rest action).
  • Specific named rest locations = potential XP-spend points if you’ve banked 7+ XP. Otherwise walking past them is fine.

Prodigy Mode — Use It If You Have It

If your copy includes Prodigy Mode (introduced in 2nd Kickstarter / 2nd edition), use it. Adds 5 strong cards to the action deck, materially better odds. Recommended until you’ve actually won a few times. Check your copy before tonight — this could be the single biggest improvement available to your group.

Tonight’s Priority Order

Hour 1 — Establish the engine

  1. Read curse card aloud. Confirm win condition. Everyone agrees.
  2. Pick one note-taker. Hand them the notebook (columns below).
  3. Check Prodigy Mode availability. If yes, add the 5 cards.
  4. First crafts: hunting weapon + fire-starting. Skip cosmetics.
  5. Find hunting/fishing location near a fire source. Base of operations.
  6. Save before pushing out.

Hour 2-3 — Push outward with discipline

  1. Move to scout (cheap). Explore only with stated purpose.
  2. Hunt every time animal tracks appear on a tile.
  3. Cook before eating whenever fire is accessible.
  4. Pool skill cards on every check ≥4.
  5. Cycle hand cards aggressively — don’t let cards die to hand limit.
  6. Stay together as a group. No splitting.
  7. Save before any check ≥7 cards or any story decision.

Hour 3+ — Wind down to a strong save

  1. When someone notices it’s late: “saving in 3 actions.”
  2. Walk to a save terrain.
  3. Save with: deck non-empty, discard reasonably clean, food banked, infrastructure (weapon/fire) preserved.

The Note-Taker’s Job

One designated player. Logger, not co-driver.

Card # Location/name Items left Exits/leads Notes

Also track: - Solved card numerics (so we don’t redo them) - Hidden numbers spotted in terrain artwork - Hunting/fishing spots used (rotation tracking) - Save points visited (backtracking reference) - Items crafted + recipe source - NPCs/encounters and what they wanted

Every 15-20 min: announce “deck check” and read the action deck count out loud, plus state counts per character. If the deck is below 30%, also call the curse-card count in discard.

Curse-Agnostic Puzzle-Solving Principles

Spoiler-free guidance that applies to any curse, compiled from community experience.

Engage with the clue card, actively

  • Your clue card is a concrete instruction set, not flavor. Every element on it — numbers, drawings, symbols, place-names, scribbles — is probably load-bearing.
  • Before starting, read every element aloud. Agree as a group what each one might mean. Write those hypotheses down. You’ll refine them as you play.
  • When a game session stalls, return to the clue card and re-examine elements you haven’t “used” yet.

Don’t auto-discard flavor-looking items

Offerings, prepared foods, trinkets, trophies, unusual tokens — if the game hands you something that seems like pure flavor, keep it. Curses often require specific items discovered hours earlier.

Distance on the clue map is unreliable

The in-game map / clue card is hand-drawn. Proportions are approximate. Especially after major terrain features, actual card-distance can diverge significantly from visual distance. Don’t trust scale — count cards.

Read ALL text, including card backs

Per designer @sgbeal in the gotchas thread: missing the back-of-card text can literally leave the game unwinnable. If a card has text on the back, read it. Multiple players have reported getting stuck for hours because they assumed back-text was decorative.

If you’re stuck, check what you haven’t tried

When a curse feels unsolvable, the problem is almost always one of: - A clue element you never decoded - A terrain you never re-explored after getting a new item - A card back you never read - An item you auto-discarded thinking it was flavor - A hidden number in terrain artwork you missed

Before quitting a session in frustration, walk through this list as a group.

Pacing reality check

Full curses are long. An experienced BGG player reported 21 hours across 6 sessions, 3 lives, still died once before eventual win. This is normal. A first-session “win” looks like:

  • Survival loop established (hunt / cook / eat)
  • Save point reached with infrastructure intact (weapon, fire-starter, food)
  • At least one meaningful clue decoded
  • Group has a shared mental model of “what we’re trying to do”

You are not expected to finish a curse in one night with 3-4 players.

Safety nets worth knowing about

  • Card 777 is an extra-life card in 2nd-edition+ copies — you can pick it before starting for an easier mode. Check your copy.
  • Buried treasure type cards occasionally grant a fresh action deck — don’t ignore dig actions when they appear.
  • Saving the game can prune used hunting cards from circulation — useful for refreshing depleted hunting spots.

State Cards — The Compounding Death Spiral

From the Serious Poulp “Dealing with State Cards” thread. This is the single most important mechanic most new players underestimate.

The compounding rule

Per @Unisus (translating the rules):

“Remember that you have to discard one card from the Action deck for each red-hand symbol you have in your hand when getting a new state with the discard symbol.”

Translation: when you acquire a new state card that has the discard symbol, you discard one card from the action deck for each red-hand-symbol state already in your hand. So:

States held New discard-symbol state acquired Action deck cost
0 1st state 0 cards lost
1 2nd state 1 card lost
2 3rd state 2 cards lost
3 4th state 3 cards lost

State accumulation is exponential pressure on the deck. This is likely why your past games “stalled out” — every new state was silently draining more cards than the previous one.

State priority guide (per @brisingre, experienced player)

“I’ll pretty much never spend more than 1 card to do it, but if I can do it for 1 card or less I try to stop them building up.”

State Severity Drop strategy
Bloody WORST in the game — drop ASAP if possible Removed by swimming. Hard to drop because swim locations are limited.
Paranoid Mild — usually free to drop Just remember it’s there. Drop when you have a free moment.
Tired Mild — wait for free removal Camp/rest action when convenient. Don’t waste cards.
Injured Moderate Craft a Splint, then treat the whole party in one sweep
Frightened Moderate Sticks around — accept it
Poison Moderate Needs a specific “Notes” card. Get the Notes card fast
Nauseous Moderate Needs a Notes card. Often lingers
Hungry / Frozen Situational Drop ASAP when convenient, usually rare
Terrified Severe As good as permanent — accept it
Frankenstein’s Monster Permanent Story-locked, can’t be removed

Operational rules for tonight

  1. Track state count out loud. Note-taker calls each character’s state count when it changes. This is how you anticipate the compounding cost.
  2. Drop free states immediately. Paranoid in particular — it costs 0 to drop in most cases. Just doing the action removes it.
  3. Don’t tank multiple states for “we’ll deal with it later.” Each new one costs more. The longer you wait, the worse the next one is.
  4. Bloody is the kill state. If anyone gets Bloody, your top priority is to find swimming water. Don’t fight, don’t explore, swim.
  5. Group failures cause Paranoid (gotcha #22). With 3-4 players, a failed group action gives the active character Paranoid — drop it next turn.
  6. One state per type, max. Drawing a duplicate = resolve effect, return old to box, take new. Don’t accumulate duplicates by mistake.

Mindset Rules (BGG community wisdom)

Direct quotes worth holding in mind at the table:

  • “You cannot win if you’re dead.” — game designer @byronczimmer. Survival > progress, every time.
  • “Curiosity kills the cursed. Stay on target.” — @randiv. Wandering for the sake of wandering is what kills past sessions.
  • “Don’t be stubborn. If you can’t move on in a direction, try to find another way or find something to help you move forward instead of wasting life.” — @karhgath. When a check fails repeatedly, walk away and find another path.
  • “It’s okay to fail. Taking a temporary setback is better than wasting cards trying to succeed.” — @karhgath. Failure on a low-stakes check < deck depletion.
  • “Every card counts — it’s your life. That ‘just one extra card’ here and there all adds up.” — @karhgath.

Reframing the Win

Tonight is not about finishing the curse. With 3-4 players over 3-5 hours, you’re aiming for: - Survive the opening - Establish the hunt/cook/eat loop - Identify the curse’s directional pull - Save mid-curse in a strong position (deck non-empty, low discard contamination, infrastructure intact, food banked)

A solid save tonight = a winnable continuation next session. That’s the actual win condition for game night.
